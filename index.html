Marie Curie

The woman who changed science forever

The Life of a Scientific Pioneer

Marie Skłodowska Curie (1867-1934) was a Polish and naturalized-French physicist and chemist who conducted pioneering research on radioactivity. She was the first woman to win a Nobel Prize, the first person to win Nobel Prizes in multiple scientific fields, and the first woman to become a professor at the University of Paris.

Her Remarkable Achievements

  • 1891 - Moved to Paris to study physics and mathematics at the University of Paris
  • 1893 - Earned a master's degree in physics
  • 1894 - Earned a master's degree in mathematics
  • 1895 - Married physicist Pierre Curie
  • 1898 - Discovered the elements polonium and radium with her husband Pierre
  • 1903 - Awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics jointly with Pierre Curie and Henri Becquerel for their work on radiation
  • 1906 - Became the first female professor at the University of Paris after Pierre's death
  • 1911 - Awarded the Nobel Prize in Chemistry for her discovery of the elements polonium and radium
  • 1914 - Helped establish the Curie Institutes in Paris and Warsaw
  • 1934 - Died from aplastic anemia, believed to be caused by exposure to radiation

"Nothing in life is to be feared, it is only to be understood. Now is the time to understand more, so that we may fear less."

— Marie Curie

A Legacy of Scientific Excellence

Marie Curie's contributions to science have been immeasurable. Her discovery of polonium and radium led to significant advances in the treatment of cancer. The development of X-rays during World War I saved countless lives. Her dedication to science, despite facing significant gender discrimination, paved the way for women in STEM fields.

Her legacy lives on through the Curie Institute, which continues to be a major cancer research center and hospital. The element curium was named in honor of Marie and Pierre Curie. Her papers from the 1890s are still considered too dangerous to handle due to their radioactivity and are kept in lead-lined boxes.
